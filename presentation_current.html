Part 1: Network Fundamentals & Theory

Building from basic concepts to theoretical frameworks

What is a Network?

Simple Definition: A network is a collection of actors and the connections between them

Examples in ECHO

  • Hub teaching spokes
  • Spokes learning from peers
  • Experts advising hub
  • Case discussions flowing between experts

Key Insight: It's not just WHO is in the network, but HOW they're connected

Basic Network Vocabulary

Term Also Called Definition ECHO Example
Node Vertex, Actor An entity in the network A clinic or hub
Edge Tie, Link, Arc A connection between nodes Teaching relationship
Degree Connectivity Number of connections a node has How many partners a clinic has

Basic Network Vocabulary

Term Also Called Definition ECHO Example
Path Route Sequence of edges connecting nodes How knowledge travels

Types of Ties (Edges)

Tie Characteristics

Directed vs Undirected
Does the relationship have direction?
Teaching: Hub → Spoke (directed)
Collaboration: Clinic ↔ Clinic (undirected)

Weighted vs Binary
Is it just yes/no or does strength matter?
Binary: Connected or not
Weighted: 10 interactions vs 2

Types of Ties (Edges)

In ECHO Networks

Teaching Ties
• Directed: Hub → Spokes
• Weighted: Session frequency
• Formal relationship

Peer Learning Ties
• Can be bidirectional
• Weighted: Interaction intensity
• Often informal

From Individual to Relational Thinking

Traditional Question: Why do some clinics perform better?
Look at: Staff size, funding, location, equipment...

Network Question: How does a clinic's connections affect performance?
Look at: Who they learn from, who they teach, their position in the network...

Common Network Structures

Star/Hub-and-Spoke

  • One central node connects all others
  • Efficient but vulnerable
  • Clear hierarchy

Common Network Structures

Distributed/Mesh

  • Many interconnections
  • Resilient but complex
  • No clear center

Common Network Structures

Small World

  • Clusters with bridges between
  • Local density + global reach
  • Six degrees of separation

Key Theoretical Frameworks

Now that we understand basic concepts, let's explore foundational theories:

Strength of Weak Ties (Granovetter, 1973)
Your distant acquaintances often provide more
novel information than close contacts
ECHO Application: Occasional peer connections may bring fresh perspectives

Key Theoretical Frameworks

Structural Holes (Burt, 1992)
Bridging disconnected groups provides information and control advantages
ECHO Application: Clinics connecting different specialties gain unique insights

Key Theoretical Frameworks

Small World Networks (Watts & Strogatz, 1998)
High clustering + short paths = optimal for diffusion and coordination
ECHO Application: Regional clusters connected through hub enable rapid spread

Key Theoretical Frameworks

Preferential Attachment (Barabási, 1999)
"Rich get richer" - well-connected nodes attract more connections
ECHO Application: Active clinics become natural peer leaders

Three Fundamental Network Questions

1. Selection or Influence?

Do similar clinics choose to connect (homophily)?
Or do connected clinics become similar (peer influence)?

Why it matters: Determines intervention strategy

Three Fundamental Network Questions

2. How Does Structure Shape Outcomes?

Which network positions lead to better performance?
How does connectivity affect adoption of best practices?

Why it matters: Identifies high-leverage positions

Three Fundamental Network Questions

3. How Do Networks Evolve?

What drives new connection formation?
Why do some relationships persist while others fade?

Why it matters: Guides network growth strategies

Key Takeaways

Theoretical Insights

  • Networks reveal invisible patterns that drive outcomes
  • Position matters as much as attributes
  • Small structural changes can have large impacts

Methodological Lessons

  • Standard statistics often inappropriate for networks
  • Multiple modeling approaches exist - choose wisely
  • Network-specific models handle dependencies properly
